Past, present and future of CS as an educational discipline

What is Computer Science?

Computer science encompasses the study of the capabilities of computers and how we use and design them to accomplish those tasks. This makes detailing the history of computer science as an educational discipline difficult; the core ideas that make modern computing possible are impossible to separate from their scientific and mathematical origins. This timeline will show (a very small subset of) the major events that most greatly impacted the advancement of computer technologies, paying special attention to events that have implications on education and related policy.

General overview of CS History

Computer Science as we know it today is a relatively modern discipline; the first degree offering for Computer Science didn't exist until 1953. However, a history of computer science would be incomplete without highlighting a few critical contributions from mathematicians and scientists that formalized ideas and advanced computing capabilities. Because of the changing focus of computing research, it is helpful to categorize the history of computing into pre-WW2, WW2-early 1970's, and mid 1970's-present. This broadly encompasses theoretical foundations, construction of efficient devices, and wider adoption of computers and their integration in modern life.

Antiquity-1940

  • Pre-1800

    A variety of devices and systems had been constructed to read input from a series of weights, levers, punched cards, and other physical media. While they could be considered to have 'programs,' these devices were never general purpose in any capacity; they were music players, crop processors, or other tools designed to produce one type of product.
  • 1834
    English matematician Charles Babbage publishes a design for a machine that can perform arbirtary mathematical operations- the Analytic Engine. While he was not able to build it before his death, this design outlined the mechanisms needed for a digital computer. Babbage's Analytic Engine is widely accepted as being the first general-purpose, digital computer in history.
  • 1843
    Ada Lovelace, a student of Babbage, wrote a series of instructions that could be run on the theoretical Analytic Engine. This is widely accepted as being the first computer program ever written.
  • 1847
    George Boole publishes a paper on the arithmetic of logic, laying the foundation for what is now called Boolean Logic in honor of this work.




Computer Technology
CS Education
  • The Complex Number Calculator, a general purpose calculating machine, is demonstrated to receive input from a remote terminal. This is the first instance of a computer that can be interacted with remotely. It couldn't store programs to run later though!
    1940
  • The Harvard Mark I is completed; this is the one of the earliest general-purpose, electromechanical computers.
    1944
  • ENIAC is completed; this is one of the first programmable, digital, electronic, general-purpose computers.

    John von Neumann writes a draft of a paper detailing an architecture for a computer that stores data and instructions in the same type of memory. This architecture is still the dominant design in computer architecture.
    1945
  • 1946
    Lectures on computing are organized and held at University of Pennsylvania's Moore school of Electrical Engineering. This event created a cohort of innovators that shaped the course of computing research.
  • 1947
    The Association of Computing Machinery, the foremost professional organization in computing, is founded. The ACM remains the most influential organization in computing research (alongside IEEE)
  • Transistors are used in a computer for the first time; this technology is what enabled the extremely rapid increase in computing power and speed of the decades that followed.
    1953
    The first Computer Science degree program is launched at Cambridge.
  • MIT begins researching keyboards to directly interface with a computer; this is the foundation of the modern computer user experience
    1956
  • Russell Kirsch creates the first digital image by scanning a photograph of his 3 month old son. The image was 176x176 pixels.
    1957
  • 1962
    Formal Computer Science education comes to US universities; Princeton begins offering a degree program in CS.
  • 1965
    The first set of students, 87 in total, matriculate through a Computer Science program in the US.
  • Semiconductors are first used in RAM; this technology enabled computers to become orders of magnitude smaller and faster.
    1966
  • ARPANET is first designed to connect remote research installations together. At the time, this network consisted of mostly US Government agencies, universities, and national research laboratories. However, this network would eventually evolve into the modern day Internet!
    1967
    First year that over 100 US students complete a CS degree (222).
  • 1968
    'Curriculum 68' is published; This is the first set of standards put forth for (post-secondary) computer science education. Modern computer science programs (including my own at Georgia Tech) continue to follow these recommedations with remarkably few changes.
  • 1970
    Over 1000 US students complete a Bachelor's degree in CS (1544)
  • Intel releases the Intel 4004; the first microprocessor ever produced. It has a clock speed of 740 KHz.
    1971
  • Xerox PARC Alto Released; First graphical, keyboard/mouse, networked computer
    1974
  • 1975
    Over 5000 US students complete a Bachelor's degree in CS (5033)
  • The Apple II is released; it has a processor speed of 1.023 MHz and 4 KB of RAM.
    1977
  • 1980
    Over 10000 US students complete a Bachelor's degree in CS (11154) For one year in the early 1980s, a survey showed that 9% of the high school graduates wanted to study computer science. If this percentage had continued, computer science would have had as many students as all of engineering! With the recent surge of interest in AI, similar situations are common to this day. (cite)
  • The IBM Personal Computer with Microsoft DOS 1.0 is released; 4.77 MHz, 16 KB of RAM.
    1981
    The Computer Programme debuts on the BBC; the Computer Literacy Project, designed to "introduce interested adults to the world of computers." The project is supported by Acorn (now ARM), who developed the BBC Microcomputer system (BBC Micro). This computer is the inspiration for a tool that many CS educators use today: the BBC Micro:bit!
  • 1983
    "A Nation at Risk" is published; in addition to calling for reforms to the education system as a whole, the report also specifically highlights the need of students to learn computing skills.
  • The Connection Machine is developed; First example of using massive parallelism to solve single large problems. This concept would prove useful in later years, once scaling from Moore's Law began to slow down.
    1986
    A record 42,337 US students gradute with a Bachelor's degree in CS. The 40k student threshold would not be reached again until 2001!
  • PowerBook laptops released; First commercially successful line of truly portable computers
    1991
    Number of CS graduates settles around 25k, where it will remain for most of the decade
  • First Apple Newton is shipped; First 'PDA'; precursor to the modern handheld smartphone
    1993
  • Pew Research begins polling American adults about their Internet usage; 52% respond that they "at least occasionally" use the Internet.
    2000
  • 2005
    Arduino is released; this small microcontroller is largely responsible for the surge of 'maker' hobbyists. Arduino remains a major player in the microcontroller market, and is the first exposure that many students have to controlling electronics via code.
  • First iPhone is released; The iPhone is now many students' first direct interaction with computers!
    2007
    Professor David Wiley of Utah State offers a course online to anyone in the world who wanted to join. This is cited as the first example of a Massive Open Online Course (MOOC)
  • 2009
    The ACM launches Computer Science Education Week
  • Only 35% of US adults report owning a smartphone. Today, that figure is now >90%.
    2011
  • Raspberry Pi is released; this is the first commercially available full computer that is designed to interface with custom electronics through GPIO pins
    2012
  • 2013
    Code.org launches first Hour of Code
  • 2014
    Georgia Tech launches their Online Master's of Science in Computer Science degree (OMSCS)
  • 2015
    The BBC launches the Micro:bit; this tiny computing platform is widely used in schools today, and is an excellent tool to get students started with physical computing without the setup, overhead, and wiring challenges of a raspberry pi.
  • 2017
    Virginia becomes the first US state to require computer science in K-12 schools.
  • 2021
    Record high number of degrees awarded this year across all levels (undergrad, master's, and Doctoral). Undergraduate degrees ararded > 100,000. Master's > 50,000. Doctoral > 18,000.
  • In a Pew Research survey, 95% of American teens report that they own a smartphone, overtaking the 90% that have desktop or laptop computers.
    2022
    Georgia joins the last few states in establishing standards for K-12 computer science education; at this point, all US states have requirements for CS courses in K-12 schools
