Future of Computer Science Education The predicament is clear; computer technology is a fundamental part of modern life, and it becomes more embedded into society by the day. If we hope to prepare children to thrive in this increasingly computerized world, our schools and governments must allocate the resources necessary to support this massive new demand on our education system. The aforementioned challenges will likely continue into the future, but there are many different approaches to mitigate the difficulty they present. These solutions require alignment in goals between students, teachers, school administrators, legislators, and community members. Here are actions that each party can do to improve the situation: Students Sign up for available computer science courses Request more computer science course offerings Request school and community support for computing related extracurricular activities such as robotics (FRC, FLL, Vex), programming clubs (Girls Who Code, Black Girls Code), and hackathons. Explore the intersection of your other interests and computing. Sports, science, music, politics, and literature all have connections to computing. Teachers For teachers of other disciplines: Learn about how computing affects your subject. There are connections everywhere! Be mindful of your attitude toward technology in the classroom; students lose respect for computer science if they hear other teachers make dismissive comments like "I'm no good with computers" or "This device never works." Prefer fostering a problem-solving mindset in these situations. People are human, and you'll never know everything. Take these frustrating moments as an opportunity to model appropriate and effective responses to technological setbacks! Collaborate with your CS teachers! Have you spoken with them? Have you asked what connections there are between your subject and computer science? For CS teachers: Strive for content mastery. Most K-12 CS teachers don't have a computer science degree, and many don't even have a degree in a related field. Be honest in assessing your gaps in knowledge, and be proactive in filling them. Demand professional development opportunities that allow you to feel confident and prepared to teach the subject Integrate other subjects! The introduction of computer technologies into nearly every aspect of modern life is an excellent opportunity to integrate student experiences in the presentation of content knowledge; this kind of linking to student experience is supported by research to be especially effective in reaching students, especially younger students. Learn about CS standards for your district, state, and country. While there is not total agreement on standards in the US, there are consistent themes that are emphasized in nearly all standards that have been published. Review them and determine what exactly the standards are requiring your students to know and to be able to do Admins Support teachers who want to add CS into their skillset! share information of professional development opportunities advocate for more if sufficient ones don't exist facilitate planning between CS and teachers from other disciplines Facilitate coordination between your CS teachers and CS teachers from other schools/districts Legislators Support initiatives that reduce the financial delta between industry and education for CS graduates Requirements for including computer science in schools are great, but only if there is investment proportional to the size of the change that schools are being asked to make Parents/Community Members Request more computer science course offerings Request school and community support for computing related extracurricular activities such as robotics (FRC, FLL, Vex), programming clubs (Girls Who Code, Black Girls Code), and hackathons. Support students in finding applications of the ideas of computer science into their daily lives, even if they don't like the subject on its own. Fundraise for resources necessary to give students opportunities to grow their skills; cloud computing subscriptions, hardware resources, etc.
