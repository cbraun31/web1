  • Baase, S. (2003). A gift of fire : Social, legal, and ethical issues for computers and the internet. Pearson.
  • This book offers a perspective on ethical issues in computing from 2003. While the examples may be lost on the current generation of students (Napster anyone?), it is eerie that the problems presented here are ones that we are still facing 20 years later. Even in the early 2000’s, Baase forecasts the effect that technology would have on education. "Both the technological advances brought about by computers and the extraordinary pace of development can have dramatic impacts on people's lives... Traditional forms of education face competition from software and electronic classrooms." (Baase, 2009, p7). 
  • With the newly available generative AI tools, a familiar concern echoes- do the conveniences of computing resources lead to a decline of human ability? "People are often willing to let computers do their thinking for them. Abdication of responsibility to exercise judgement, and sometimes, a reasonable amount of skepticism, has serious consequences." (Baase, 2003, p376). I have already seen these consequences in my short time in the classroom. One assignment involved students facing an object East. Unsure of which way that was, they turned to Google for help, only for the first image to have east and west reversed! There is a balance that needs to be struck between taking advantage of the wealth of knowledge available to us and verifying that what we find online is corroborated elsewhere.
  • Bates, B. (2019). Learning theories simplified...and how to apply them to teaching (2nd ed.). Sage.
  • In Chapter 16, the authors review Cognitivism from the perspective of Vygotsky’s work. The principles, aside from being generally well regarded by the research community, also mirror content from computer science. For example. “Break the given task into smaller sub-tasks” (p46) is also fundamental for students to learn as they approach computing problems. This affords an excellent opportunity to allow students to work with a Most Knowledgeable Other to break the problem down together. The students simultaneously benefit from reinforcing content knowledge and building experience in the process of breaking down problems.

  • While the purpose of this book is to share humorous and interesting anecdotes, making Richard Feynman the subject leads to many unintentional insights. Many of his thoughts can be backed up by learning theorists. For example, take this quote on his needing motivation to learn a skill "I mean, if I were doing any mathematical thing at all, I would find some practical example for which it would be useful." (p30). Framing material in the context of what the learner will be able to accomplish with this skill is a popular principle. On learning by doing difficult work, Feynman stumbles into something that echoes Vygotsky’s Zone of Proximal Development; "That was a very good way to get educated, working on the senior problems and learning how to pronounce things."   • Fisher, D., & Frey, N. (2021). Better learning through structured teaching. Alexandria, Va, Usa Ascd.
  • This book covers the “Gradual Release of Responsibility” model of instruction. The idea is that simply lecturing is ineffective and not engaging, but students will be lost without some sort of guidance at the start. The approach offers 4 stages: I do, we do, you do together, you do alone. 
  • “Focused instruction is typically done with the whole class and usually lasts 15 minutes or less…” (p5). This is an area that I struggle with in my classroom. Because I feel confident in my ability to tutor 1 on 1 and struggle with classroom management, there are many times when I send my students to do a task without first giving them a solid enough foundation to succeed. One main challenge I have in implementing this in my classroom is design and performing of formative assessments to ensure that my students have learned enough to get started.
